# Le gène du raconteur: Why We Can't Stop Making Stuff Up

By Thorvaldur Heimskarsson

## Synopsis

Human beings are the only animals known to tell stories about things that never happened, to people who never existed, for reasons they barely understand.

Or are they?

In Le gène du raconteur, the world-unrenowned Icelandic essayist Thorvaldur Heimskarsson advances a provocative thesis: fiction is not a cultural invention but a cognitive necessity. Long before humanity painted cave walls, wrote epics, founded religions, drafted constitutions, or posted dubious claims online, the human brain had already become a storytelling machine.

Drawing from psychology, anthropology, neuroscience, history, linguistics, and several regrettable family gatherings, Heimskarsson argues that humans do not merely tell stories—we think in stories. Narrative is not a decorative layer applied to reality after the fact; it is the operating system through which reality becomes comprehensible at all.

Why do children invent imaginary friends? Why do eyewitnesses confidently remember events that never occurred? Why do nations construct founding myths, investors explain market crashes after they happen, and internet commenters develop complete theories from three blurry pixels? According to Heimskarsson, these are not separate phenomena but manifestations of the same deeply human impulse: the compulsion to transform chaotic events into coherent narratives.

The book explores how storytelling shaped human evolution, why conspiracy theories feel psychologically satisfying, how memory functions less like an archive and more like an overworked novelist, and why every person's explanation of their own life is, at best, a carefully edited work of historical fiction.

Along the way, Heimskarsson dismantles the comforting distinction between "fiction" and "reality." The stories we knowingly invent differ only in degree from the stories we unconsciously construct every day to explain our careers, relationships, identities, politics, and place in the universe. Even science, he suggests, progresses by replacing one narrative with another that survives contact with evidence a little longer.

Neither cynical nor nihilistic, Le gène du raconteur concludes that humanity's habit of making things up is not a flaw to be corrected but a feature to be understood. Our greatest mistakes emerge from stories believed too strongly. Our greatest achievements emerge from stories shared widely enough.

Civilizations are built from stone, steel, and concrete.

But first, someone has to make up a story.

## What this is about

This whole project consists an elaborate joke where the contents, authorship, the reader's reaction after reaching the AI disclosure, all constitutes as a proof of the thesis: Le gène du raconteur. 

### Le gène du raconteur

The book argues that humans think in narratives, which practically writes itself because every chapter can be built around a familiar paradox:
* Memory is storytelling.
* History is storytelling.
* Identity is storytelling.
* Religion is storytelling.
* Economics is storytelling.
* Science is storytelling with unusually strict editors.
* AI is storytelling without consciousness.
* The reader's understanding of this book is itself storytelling.

### The jab at the end of the chapter

The last chapter ends with AI disclosure that's something along in this vibe:

> You have spent the last six hours reading a book about why humans invent authors.
> We invented one for you.

The reader cannot even protest without proving the point. The moment they encountered "Thorvaldur Heimskarsson," they started building a narrative object in their head. They inferred age, nationality, temperament, education, motives, credibility, and worldview from a few words on a page.

### The uncomfortable question

The AI disclosure must bring an epistemic discomfort.

once a book exists, readers mostly interact with the book, not the author.

People like to imagine books as a transfer of wisdom from Author Brain™ to Reader Brain™, but that's not how reading feels. Reading feels like your own brain running a simulation. The author just supplies prompts.

Which is very much in line with your proposed thesis.

Suppose someone reads Le gène du raconteur on your blog and genuinely finds a chapter insightful. What exactly happened?

* You had an idea.
* An AI helped turn it into prose.
* The reader reconstructed the idea inside their own head.
* The reader attributed that insight to "Thorvaldur Heimskarsson."

At which step did the insight become fake?

That's the uncomfortable question.

### The gimmick that surpasses existing AI-authored books

Historically we've attached enormous importance to authorship because authors were difficult to manufacture. A book implied years of labor by a specific human. AI breaks that assumption. Now the scarce thing isn't prose production but having an interesting idea worth reading about.

Most AI-generated books fail not because they're AI-generated but because they're boring. They are 50,000 words in search of a reason to exist.

This fake Icelandic raconteur already has a stronger central gimmick than most of them:

* A serious-sounding popular science book.
* About humanity's tendency to invent narratives.
* Written by a fictional scholar.
* Generated with AI.
* Revealed at the end.

That's at least a coherent joke. It has structure. It has a payoff.
