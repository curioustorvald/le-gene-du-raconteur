## A note for the jurisdictionally curious

The split above is not merely whimsical; it tracks a genuine and unsettled area
of law, which the book has the good fortune to be about.

- **United States.** *Thaler v. Perlmutter* (D.C. Cir. 2025; cert. denied 2026)
  and the U.S. Copyright Office's guidance establish that purely AI-generated
  material, produced without sufficient human control over its expression, is
  uncopyrightable. Part 1 is therefore public domain with or without this
  dedication; CC0 simply removes all doubt.

- **United Kingdom.** Section 9(3) of the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act
  1988 takes the opposite tack: for a "computer-generated" work having no human
  author, it deems the author to be "the person by whom the arrangements
  necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken" — inventing an author,
  by statute, in precisely the circumstance where none exists. (Term: 50 years;
  no moral rights; and contested for modern generative AI.)

Which is, of course, the entire thesis of the book — that when there is no
author, we conjure one, because the system cannot proceed without one — now
enacted by the law itself. We could not have arranged it better, and did not
try.
