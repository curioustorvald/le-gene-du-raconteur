## Front matter

## Ch.1 — The Storytelling Animal
- [v] Jonathan Gottschall, *The Storytelling Animal: How Stories Make Us Human* (Houghton
  Mifflin Harcourt, 2012). Thesis: humans are the storytelling animal; fiction as
  cognitive/evolutionary feature.
- [v] Robin Dunbar, *Grooming, Gossip and the Evolution of Language* (Harvard UP / Faber,
  1996). Language evolved as "vocal grooming"; social-brain hypothesis; ~150 ("Dunbar's
  number") as cognitive limit on stable relationships. (Group size ↔ neocortex ratio.)
- [v] Brian Boyd, *On the Origin of Stories: Evolution, Cognition, and Fiction* (Belknap
  Press of Harvard UP, 2009). Art/fiction as human adaptation derived from play.
- [v] Yuval Noah Harari, *Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind* (Hebrew 2011; English
  ed. Harvill Secker, 2014). "Imagined orders" / collective fictions (money, nations,
  laws, gods, corporations) enable flexible large-scale cooperation among strangers.

## Ch.2 — The Memory That Lies
- [v] Frederic C. Bartlett, *Remembering: A Study in Experimental and Social Psychology*
  (Cambridge UP, 1932). "War of the Ghosts": repeated/serial reproduction; recollection
  distorted via assimilation, levelling, sharpening to fit schema (canoes→boats,
  hunting→fishing). Memory as active reconstruction, not playback.
- [v] Elizabeth Loftus & John Palmer (1974), "Reconstruction of Automobile Destruction."
  Verb in question ("smashed" vs "hit") raised speed estimates; "smashed" group more
  likely to falsely recall broken glass a week later (none was present).
- [v] Elizabeth Loftus & Jacqueline Pickrell (1995), "The Formation of False Memories"
  ("lost in the mall"). 24 participants (U. Washington); ~25% came to "remember," fully or
  partially, a fabricated childhood event of being lost in a shopping mall.
- [v] Ulric Neisser & Nicole Harsch (1992), "Phantom flashbulbs: False recollections of
  hearing the news about Challenger" (in *Affect and Accuracy in Recall*, Cambridge UP).
  Questionnaire day after the 28 Jan 1986 explosion, repeated ~2.5–3 yrs later; high
  confidence, low accuracy. Flashbulb memories special in *perceived* accuracy, not actual.

## Ch.3 — The Self Who Narrates
- [v] Michael Gazzaniga (with Roger Sperry; "interpreter" coined w/ Joseph LeDoux, 1978).
  Split-brain experiments at Caltech. Chicken-claw (to left hemisphere) / snow-shovel (to
  right hemisphere): patient picks chicken with right hand, shovel with left; asked why,
  the speaking left hemisphere — blind to the snow scene — confabulates: "the chicken claw
  goes with the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed." Left-brain
  "interpreter" invents reasons without access to true causes.
- [v] Daniel C. Dennett (1992), "The Self as a Center of Narrative Gravity" (in *Self and
  Consciousness: Multiple Perspectives*, eds. Kessel, Cole & Johnson; Lawrence Erlbaum).
  The self is an abstract object — like a centre of gravity, real in its effects, not a
  thing you can point to — a useful fiction the brain spins from words and deeds.
- [v] Oliver Sacks, *The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat* (1985), ch. "A Matter of
  Identity": William Thompson, Korsakoff's syndrome, "confabulatory delirium" — ceaselessly
  invents identities for himself and others (Sacks as customer, butcher, friend "Tom
  Pitkins"...), improvising a self by the second because memory cannot supply one.
- [v] Dan P. McAdams (life-story theory; McAdams & McLean, "Narrative Identity," *Current
  Directions in Psychological Science*, 2013). Narrative identity = "the internalized and
  evolving story of the self"; "self-as-author" emerges in adolescence; redemptive life
  stories correlate with well-being. Level 3 of his personality model.

## Ch.4 — The Authorized Version
- [v] E. H. Carr, *What Is History?* (1961; Trevelyan Lectures, Cambridge). Facts of the
  past are not "like fish on the fishmonger's slab" but "fish swimming about in a vast...
  ocean"; what the historian catches depends on where he fishes and what tackle he uses.
  "Study the historian before you begin to study the facts." History = interpretation,
  selection.
- [v] Hayden White, *Metahistory: The Historical Imagination in Nineteenth-Century Europe*
  (Johns Hopkins UP, 1973). Historians employ "emplotment"; modes (after Northrop Frye):
  romance, tragedy, comedy, satire. Historians as artists imposing narrative form/meaning.
- [v] Benedict Anderson, *Imagined Communities: Reflections on the Origin and Spread of
  Nationalism* (Verso, 1983). The nation is an "imagined community" — members never meet
  yet share an image of communion; enabled by "print capitalism" (vernacular print).
- [v] Eric Hobsbawm & Terence Ranger (eds.), *The Invention of Tradition* (Cambridge UP,
  1983). Many "ancient" traditions are recent fabrications; Hugh Trevor-Roper's chapter
  "The Highland Tradition of Scotland": the modern kilt an 18th-c invention (c.1730, devised
  by an Englishman as workwear), "clan tartans" a 19th-c invention.
- [ref] Iceland (author's own; common knowledge): Alþingi founded c.930 AD at Þingvellir
  (among the oldest surviving parliaments); Sagas of Icelanders written 13th–14th c. about
  the 9th–10th-c settlement era; Ingólfr Arnarson traditionally the first permanent Norse
  settler (c.874).

## Ch.5 — Gods and Other Good Stories
- [v] Stewart E. Guthrie, *Faces in the Clouds: A New Theory of Religion* (OUP, 1993).
  Religion as systematic anthropomorphism; we respond to environmental ambiguity with
  human-/animal-like models — "best bet" perceptual strategy (faces in clouds).
- [v] Justin L. Barrett, HADD = Hyperactive/Hypersensitive Agency Detection Device (in
  *Why Would Anyone Believe in God?*, 2004). Agency-detection biased to fire on minimal
  cues / non-inertial motion; better to mistake wind for a tiger than a tiger for wind.
- [v] Pascal Boyer, *Religion Explained: The Evolutionary Origins of Religious Thought*
  (Basic Books, 2001). Religion as cognitive *byproduct* (not adaptation); "minimally
  counterintuitive" (MCI) concepts — agents that are mostly normal (have minds, intentions)
  but breach one expectation (invisible, immortal, omniscient): memorable & transmissible.
- [ref] Iceland huldufólk ("hidden people"/elves) folklore; occasional rerouting of road
  projects around "elf rocks" (author's own / well-documented Icelandic cultural motif).

## Ch.6 — The Pattern That Wasn't There
- [v] Michael Shermer, *The Believing Brain: From Ghosts and Gods to Politics and
  Conspiracies—How We Construct Beliefs and Reinforce Them as Truths* (Times Books, 2011).
  Patternicity = "the tendency to find meaningful patterns in both meaningful and
  meaningless data." Agenticity = "the tendency to infuse patterns with meaning, intention,
  and agency." We form beliefs first, justify after.
- [v] Klaus Conrad (1958), *Die beginnende Schizophrenie* — coined "apophenia": the
  "unmotivated seeing of connections" with a "specific experience of abnormal
  meaningfulness." Stages: trema → apophany → anastrophe. (Greek apo + phainein.)
- [v] B. F. Skinner (1948), "'Superstition' in the Pigeon," *Journal of Experimental
  Psychology*. Random-interval feeding → pigeons develop ritualistic "superstitious"
  behaviours as if causally linked to food, though no contingency exists.
- [ref] Pareidolia (faces in noise — Man in the Moon, "Face on Mars," faces in toast);
  proportionality / major-event-needs-major-cause bias; unfalsifiability of conspiracy
  (absence of evidence reframed as evidence of cover-up). General/well-established.

## Ch.7 — The Confidence of Markets
- [v] Robert J. Shiller, *Narrative Economics: How Stories Go Viral and Drive Major
  Economic Events* (Princeton UP, 2019). Nobel laureate. Contagious economic narratives
  spread like epidemics and drive booms/panics; examples: Bitcoin, the gold standard, the
  Laffer curve, "this time is different." Narratives long ignored as "unscientific."
- [v] Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (FSG, 2011). WYSIATI ("What You See Is
  All There Is"), a System 1 feature; the "illusion of understanding" — hindsight makes
  the unforeseeable look inevitable; coherent stories built from limited visible info.
- [v] Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* (Random
  House, 2007). Coined "narrative fallacy": we impose oversimplified causal stories on the
  past, creating an illusion of inevitability and hiding the role of luck. Black-swan events.
- [v] Iceland 2008 crisis: three banks (Glitnir, Landsbanki, Kaupthing) collapsed in early
  Oct 2008; combined assets ~10–11× GDP (grew ~100%→~1000% of GDP, 2004–2008); Glitnir
  nationalisation announced 29 Sept 2008. "Útrásarvíkingar" ("outvasion/raiding vikings")
  = the credit-fuelled Icelandic financiers (bought Debenhams, Hamleys, etc.); term invokes
  the Settlement-era Viking "golden age" (medievalism/nationalism). [verified via web]

## Ch.8 — The Story with Strict Editors
- [v] Thomas S. Kuhn, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (Univ. of Chicago Press,
  1962). Paradigm; "normal science" = puzzle-solving under a reigning paradigm; anomalies
  accumulate → crisis → paradigm shift / scientific revolution. Science advances not by
  smooth accumulation but by replacing one governing framework with another (Ptolemy→
  Copernicus; Newton→Einstein).
- [v] Karl Popper, falsifiability as the demarcation criterion (*The Logic of Scientific
  Discovery*, 1934/Eng. 1959; *Conjectures and Refutations*, 1963). Asymmetry: no number of
  confirmations verifies a universal law, but one genuine counter-instance falsifies it. A
  scientific theory must "forbid" something — must be killable by a possible observation.
  Knowledge grows by bold conjectures + severe attempts at refutation.
- [v] Max Planck, *Scientific Autobiography and Other Papers* (1949; Eng. trans. F. Gaynor):
  "A new scientific truth does not triumph by convincing its opponents and making them see
  the light, but rather because its opponents eventually die, and a new generation grows up
  that is familiar with it." (Popular paraphrase: "science advances one funeral at a time.")

## Ch.9 — The Machine That Tells Stories Without Meaning Them
- [v] Claude E. Shannon (1948), "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," *Bell System
  Technical Journal*. EXACT: "These semantic aspects of communication are irrelevant to the
  engineering problem. The significant aspect is that the actual message is one selected
  from a set of possible messages." (p. 379). Founding amputation of meaning from information.
- [v] Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, "Shmitchell" [Margaret
  Mitchell] (2021), "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?
  🦜" (FAccT '21, pp. 610–623). EXACT: an LM is "a system for haphazardly stitching together
  sequences of linguistic forms it has observed in its vast training data, according to
  probabilistic information about how they combine, but without any reference to meaning:
  a stochastic parrot."
- [v] John Searle (1980), "Minds, Brains, and Programs," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*.
  Chinese Room: syntax is neither sufficient for nor constitutive of semantics; running a
  program (symbol manipulation) cannot by itself produce understanding.
- [v] Harry G. Frankfurt, *On Bullshit* (essay 1986; Princeton UP book, 2005). The liar is
  "inescapably concerned with truth-values" (must know the truth to conceal it); the
  bullshitter is *indifferent* to truth — "indifference to how things really are" is the
  essence; hence bullshit is "a greater enemy of the truth than lies are."
