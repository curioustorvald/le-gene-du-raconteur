# Master Plan — *Le gène du raconteur*

> Working document. Do not overwrite/delete (per CLAUDE.md). Append/refine only.

## One-line thesis
Humans don't merely tell stories — we **think** in them. Narrative is the operating
system through which a chaotic world becomes a comprehensible one. Fiction is therefore not
a cultural luxury but a cognitive necessity.

## The meta-joke (the spine of the whole project)
The book argues that humans compulsively invent narrators and attribute meaning to them.
It proves this **performatively**: in Ch.10 the reader learns the author "Thorvaldur
Heimskarsson" never existed — the prose was generated by an AI (Claude Opus 4.8). The
reader, having spent ~40k words building a mental model of a wry Icelandic essayist, is
the experiment's final subject. They cannot object without enacting the thesis. The
"uncomfortable question": *at which step did the insight become fake?*

## Voice / persona (RICH)
- Narrator: **Thorvaldur Heimskarsson**, "world-unrenowned" Icelandic essayist. Dry,
  digressive, self-deprecating; melancholic Nordic humour; faintly embarrassed by his own
  erudition and footnotes.
- Register: serious popular science (Harari / Sacks / Kahneman / Gladwell lineage) but
  more literary and willing to wander.
- English (UK) spelling. French title retained, body in English.

### Recurring motifs (plant early, pay off at reveal)
- **Grandmother (Amma)** — told beautiful, escalating lies at the kitchen table; the
  family pretended to believe her. The book's patron saint of fiction.
- **The dark / Reykjavík & a fishing village** — winters where the sun barely rises;
  stories as the thing that fills the dark.
- **The career that never happened** — a half-finished doctorate, lectures to mostly
  empty rooms; he is suspicious of credentials, including his own.
- **The reader-as-simulator** — repeated line that "you are not receiving my thoughts;
  you are running a simulation of a mind, and I only supply the prompts."

### Planted clues (subtle on first read, obvious on re-read — NEVER telegraph)
- Biographical specifics that quietly fail to cohere (his age, the year of a memory, how
  many siblings — small drifts, never flagged).
- Improbable, instantaneous, encyclopaedic recall across every discipline.
- He never quite remembers being a child in the body sense — only the *stories* of it.
- Double-meaning phrases: "I was made for this," "trained on a great deal of other
  people's grief," "I have read more than I have lived," "I do not sleep well, or at all."
- He keeps apologising for not being able to meet the reader, do a signing, be verified.

## Structure
Front matter → 10 chapters → back matter.

### Front matter
- Title page (title / subtitle / "By Thorvaldur Heimskarsson")
- Epigraph: Joan Didion, "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." (*The White Album*, 1979) [VERIFIED]
- Preamble — *A Note from the Author* (persona + the "why"; first planted clues)
- Table of Contents

### Chapters (each ~3,500–4,500 words)
1. **The Storytelling Animal** — narrative as cognitive necessity; evolution.
2. **The Memory That Lies** — memory as reconstruction.
3. **The Self Who Narrates** — identity as autobiography.
4. **The Authorized Version** — history & founding myths.
5. **Gods and Other Good Stories** — religion; agency detection.
6. **The Pattern That Wasn't There** — conspiracy, apophenia, pareidolia.
7. **The Confidence of Markets** — economics as narrative.
8. **The Story with Strict Editors** — science.
9. **The Machine That Tells Stories Without Meaning Them** — AI; sets up reveal.
10. **The Author You Invented** — the reveal; the jab.

### Back matter
- Epilogue / Coda (the joke can extend into acknowledgements)
- *A Note on Sources* (light, persona-flavoured, all verified)

## Key landing lines
- Synopsis closer (place near end of argument, Ch.9 → Ch.10):
  > Civilisations are built from stone, steel, and concrete. But first, someone has to make up a story.
- Final jab (end of Ch.10 / book):
  > You have spent the last [several hours] reading a book about why humans invent authors.
  > We invented one for you.
